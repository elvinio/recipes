# Beef Stir-Fry with Oyster Sauce

> Serves 4 | 25 minutes | Thai | dinner

## Ingredients

- 600 g beef sirloin or flank steak, thinly sliced against the grain into 5 mm strips
- 3 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tbsp fish sauce
- 1 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tsp dark soy sauce
- 1 tsp sugar
- 1 tsp cornflour
- 3 tbsp vegetable oil
- 5 cloves garlic, minced
- 2 long red chillies, sliced diagonally
- 200 g broccolini, cut into 5 cm pieces
- 100 g baby corn, halved lengthwise
- 150 g oyster mushrooms, torn
- 2 tbsp water
- 4 spring onions, cut into 4 cm pieces
- 800 g cooked jasmine rice, to serve

## Steps

1. Combine oyster sauce, fish sauce, soy sauce, dark soy sauce, and sugar in a bowl to make the sauce. In a separate small bowl, toss the beef strips with cornflour and 1 tsp of the soy sauce from the sauce bowl. Set both aside.
2. Heat 1.5 tbsp oil in a large wok over very high heat until smoking. Add the beef in a single layer and leave undisturbed for 1 minute to sear. Toss and cook for a further 30–60 seconds until just cooked through and slightly caramelised. Remove and set aside.
3. Wipe the wok clean with paper towels if there is excessive browning. Add remaining 1.5 tbsp oil over high heat.
4. Add garlic and chillies and fry for 30 seconds. Add broccolini and baby corn with 2 tbsp water and stir-fry for 2 minutes until the broccolini is bright green and just tender.
5. Add the oyster mushrooms and toss for 1 minute.
6. Return the beef to the wok. Pour the sauce over everything and toss vigorously for 1 minute over high heat until the sauce glazes all the ingredients.
7. Add the spring onions, toss once more, and serve immediately over jasmine rice.

## Special Notice

> Slicing beef against the grain shortens the muscle fibres and makes each piece noticeably more tender — look for the direction the fibres run and cut perpendicular to them. Briefly coating the beef in cornflour before searing helps lock in juices and gives the meat a slightly silky texture. Cook the beef in batches if your wok is small to ensure proper searing rather than steaming.
