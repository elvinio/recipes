# Prawn and Glass Noodle Salad Yam Woon Sen

> Serves 4 | 25 minutes | Thai | lunch

## Ingredients

- 400 g raw prawns, peeled and deveined
- 120 g dried glass noodles (mung bean vermicelli)
- 3 tbsp fresh lime juice (about 2 limes)
- 3 tbsp fish sauce
- 1 tsp sugar
- 4–5 bird's eye chillies, finely sliced
- 4 shallots, thinly sliced
- 3 stalks celery, thinly sliced on the diagonal, leaves reserved
- 150 g cherry tomatoes, halved
- 1 medium carrot, julienned or grated
- 1 large handful fresh coriander
- 1 large handful fresh mint leaves
- 3 spring onions, thinly sliced
- 2 tbsp roasted peanuts, roughly chopped
- Lettuce leaves, to serve (optional)

## Steps

1. Soak the glass noodles in cold water for 15 minutes until pliable. Drain and cut into roughly 10 cm lengths with scissors.
2. Bring a small saucepan of water to a boil. Blanch the soaked noodles for 1–2 minutes until just tender but still slightly firm. Drain immediately and rinse under cold water to stop cooking. Set aside.
3. In the same saucepan of boiling water, cook the prawns for 1–2 minutes until just pink and curled. Drain and run briefly under cold water to cool. Do not overcook.
4. Whisk together lime juice, fish sauce, sugar, and sliced bird's eye chillies in a large mixing bowl to make the dressing. Taste — it should be intensely sour, salty, and hot.
5. Add the glass noodles to the dressing and toss to coat.
6. Add the prawns, shallots, celery, carrot, cherry tomatoes, and spring onions. Toss gently to combine everything in the dressing.
7. Fold through the fresh coriander, mint, and celery leaves. Plate on lettuce leaves if desired. Top with chopped peanuts and serve immediately.

## Special Notice

> This salad is best served fresh — the noodles absorb the dressing quickly and the herbs wilt within 30 minutes. If preparing ahead, keep the dressing, noodles, and herbs separate and toss together just before serving. Adjust the dressing balance to taste: more lime for brightness, more fish sauce for depth, more chilli for heat. Glass noodles become translucent and slippery when cooked — cutting them into shorter lengths makes the salad easier to eat.
