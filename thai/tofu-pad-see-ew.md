# Tofu Pad See Ew

> Serves 4 | 25 minutes | Thai | dinner

## Ingredients

- 400 g fresh wide rice noodles (sen yai), gently separated
- 400 g firm tofu, pressed and cut into 2 cm cubes
- 3 tbsp dark soy sauce
- 2 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce (or vegetarian oyster sauce)
- 1 tsp white sugar
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 3 tbsp vegetable oil
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 4 eggs
- 200 g Chinese broccoli (gai lan), cut into 5 cm pieces, stems and leaves separated
- 2 tbsp water
- White pepper, to serve
- Sliced chilli in vinegar, to serve

## Steps

1. Whisk together dark soy sauce, light soy sauce, oyster sauce, sugar, and sesame oil in a small bowl to make the sauce. Set aside.
2. Press the tofu between paper towels for 10 minutes to remove excess moisture, then cut into cubes. Pat dry thoroughly — this is the key to getting crispy tofu.
3. Heat 2 tbsp oil in a large wok or heavy frying pan over high heat until smoking. Add the tofu in a single layer and fry without stirring for 2–3 minutes per side until golden and crispy. Remove and set aside.
4. Add the remaining 1 tbsp oil to the hot wok. Add the garlic and fry for 20 seconds. Add the Chinese broccoli stems and stir-fry for 1 minute, then add the leaves and 2 tbsp water. Toss for 30 seconds until wilted.
5. Add the fresh rice noodles to the wok, gently pulling them apart as you go. Pour over the sauce and toss to coat, cooking for 1–2 minutes until the noodles are well coated and beginning to char at the edges.
6. Push noodles to the side. Crack the eggs into the empty space and scramble for 30 seconds, then fold into the noodles before fully set.
7. Return the crispy tofu to the wok and toss gently to combine. Serve immediately, with white pepper and chilli in vinegar on the side.

## Special Notice

> Fresh wide rice noodles are sold refrigerated at Asian grocery stores; if unavailable, soak dried wide rice noodles in cold water for 30 minutes and drain before using. The key to authentic pad see ew flavour is allowing the noodles to sit undisturbed for 30–60 seconds on high heat to develop a lightly charred, smoky taste (known as wok hei). A good non-stick or well-seasoned wok works best.
