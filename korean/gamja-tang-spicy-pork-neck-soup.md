# Gamja Tang Spicy Pork Neck Soup

> Serves 4 | 55 minutes | Korean | dinner

## Ingredients

- 1.2 kg pork neck bones (gamsun), cut into 5–6 cm pieces
- 3 medium potatoes (about 500 g total), peeled and halved
- 3 tbsp gochugaru
- 2 tbsp gochujang
- 2 tbsp doenjang
- 2 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp sesame oil
- 6 garlic cloves, minced
- 1 tsp freshly grated ginger
- 1 tsp ground perilla seeds (deulkkae-garu) or 1 tbsp sesame seeds, ground (optional but authentic)
- 1 medium brown onion, roughly diced
- 150 g napa cabbage (wombok), roughly chopped
- 1.2 L water
- 4 spring onions, cut into 4 cm pieces
- Salt, to taste

## Steps

1. Place the pork neck bones in a large pot, cover with cold water, and bring to a boil. Blanch for 5 minutes to remove impurities. Drain and rinse the bones under cold running water. This step removes blood and reduces gaminess.
2. Combine the gochugaru, gochujang, doenjang, soy sauce, sesame oil, garlic, ginger, and ground perilla seeds (if using) in a bowl to form the seasoning paste.
3. Transfer the blanched bones to the electric pressure cooker. Add the onion, seasoning paste, and 1.2 L of fresh water. Stir to distribute the paste.
4. Seal the pressure cooker lid. Set to high pressure and cook for 30 minutes. Allow natural pressure release for 10 minutes, then carefully quick-release any remaining pressure.
5. Open the lid. Add the potatoes and napa cabbage to the broth. Set the pressure cooker to the sauté/simmer function (or transfer to a stovetop pot) and cook uncovered for 10–15 minutes until the potatoes are completely tender.
6. Taste and adjust salt. Ladle generously into deep bowls, ensuring each serving has bones, potato, and cabbage. Top with spring onions and serve with steamed rice and kimchi alongside.

## Special Notice

> The blanching in step 1 is the most important step for a clean, clear broth — never skip it. The pork neck bones are meant to be eaten: the meat pulls off the bone easily after pressure cooking and the collagen-rich marrow adds incredible richness to the soup. Gamja tang traditionally includes perilla seeds, which add a nutty depth; ground sesame seeds are a good substitute if unavailable.
