# Chicken Fricassee

> Serves 4 | 40 minutes | French | dinner

## Ingredients

- 8 bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs (about 1.2 kg total)
- 250 g button mushrooms, quartered
- 1 medium onion, finely diced
- 2 garlic cloves, minced
- 200 mL dry white wine
- 300 mL chicken stock
- 150 mL heavy cream
- 2 tbsp unsalted butter
- 1 tbsp plain flour
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- 2 sprigs fresh tarragon, leaves stripped and chopped
- Juice of 1/2 lemon
- Salt and white pepper to taste

## Steps

1. Pat the chicken thighs dry with paper towels and season all over with salt and white pepper. Heat the olive oil and 1 tbsp butter in a wide, heavy saucepan over medium-high heat.
2. Place the chicken thighs skin-side down and cook undisturbed for 6 minutes until the skin is deep golden. Flip and cook for 3 more minutes. Transfer to a plate — the chicken does not need to be cooked through at this stage.
3. Reduce heat to medium. Add the remaining butter, then the onion, mushrooms, and garlic. Cook, stirring occasionally, for 5 minutes until the onion is soft and the mushrooms have released their liquid.
4. Sprinkle the flour over the vegetables and stir for 1 minute. Pour in the white wine and bring to a bubble, scraping up the browned bits from the base.
5. Add the chicken stock and stir well. Return the chicken thighs to the pan skin-side up. Bring to a gentle simmer.
6. Cover and cook over low heat for 18 minutes until the chicken is cooked through and the sauce has thickened. Remove the lid for the last 5 minutes to concentrate the sauce slightly.
7. Transfer the chicken to a warm plate. Stir the heavy cream and most of the tarragon into the sauce. Squeeze in the lemon juice and stir to combine. Taste and adjust seasoning.
8. Return the chicken to the sauce to warm through for 1 minute. Serve scattered with the remaining fresh tarragon, alongside buttered egg noodles or steamed rice.

## Special Notice

> A fricassee differs from a braise in that the meat is gently simmered rather than long-cooked — the result is a silky, cream-laced sauce rather than a deeply reduced braise. Do not rush the initial skin-browning step, as it builds the flavour base for the entire dish.
