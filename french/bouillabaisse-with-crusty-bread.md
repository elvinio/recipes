# Bouillabaisse with Crusty Bread

> Serves 4 | 55 minutes | French | dinner

## Ingredients

- 400 g firm white fish fillets (such as monkfish or sea bass), cut into large chunks
- 300 g shell-on prawns
- 300 g mussels, scrubbed and debearded
- 1 medium fennel bulb, thinly sliced, fronds reserved
- 1 large onion, finely diced
- 4 garlic cloves, minced
- 400 g can crushed tomatoes
- 200 mL dry white wine
- 600 mL fish stock
- 3 tbsp extra virgin olive oil
- 1/2 tsp saffron threads, soaked in 2 tbsp warm water
- 1 tsp smoked paprika
- Zest of 1 orange
- 1 baguette or crusty sourdough loaf, sliced and toasted, to serve
- Salt and black pepper to taste

## Steps

1. Heat the olive oil in a large, deep saucepan over medium heat. Add the fennel, onion, and a pinch of salt. Cook, stirring, for 8 minutes until softened and beginning to caramelise.
2. Add the garlic and smoked paprika and cook for 1 minute until fragrant. Pour in the white wine and let it reduce by half, about 3 minutes.
3. Add the crushed tomatoes, fish stock, saffron with its soaking liquid, and orange zest. Stir well and bring to a vigorous simmer. Cook uncovered for 15 minutes to develop the flavour base.
4. Meanwhile, make a quick rouille: in a small bowl, combine 4 tbsp mayonnaise, 1 small minced garlic clove, a pinch of saffron, and 1/2 tsp paprika. Spread onto the toasted bread slices before serving.
5. Reduce the heat to medium. Add the firm fish chunks to the simmering broth and cook for 4 minutes.
6. Add the prawns and mussels to the pot. Cover and cook for 4–5 minutes until the prawns are pink throughout and all the mussels have opened. Discard any mussels that remain closed.
7. Taste the broth and adjust seasoning generously with salt and pepper. The broth should be rich, deeply savoury, and fragrant with saffron and fennel.
8. Ladle into deep bowls, making sure each bowl gets a generous mix of fish and shellfish. Scatter with reserved fennel fronds. Serve with rouille-topped toasted bread for dipping.

## Special Notice

> Traditional bouillabaisse is served in two courses — the broth first with croutons, then the fish — but serving it all together works beautifully for a home meal. Discard any mussels that do not open after cooking, as they may not be safe to eat.
