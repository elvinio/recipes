# Spicy Sichuan Fish Shui Zhu Yu

> Serves 4 | 40 minutes | Chinese | dinner

## Ingredients

- 700 g firm white fish fillets (basa, tilapia, or cod), sliced 1 cm thick on the diagonal
- 1 egg white
- 2 tsp cornstarch
- 1 tsp salt
- 200 g bean sprouts
- 200 g baby spinach or Chinese mustard greens
- 5 tbsp neutral oil, divided
- 20 g dried Sichuan red chilies (about 15–20 pieces)
- 2 tsp whole Sichuan peppercorns
- 4 tbsp doubanjiang (Pixian chili bean paste)
- 6 cloves garlic, minced
- 30 g fresh ginger, minced
- 600 mL chicken or fish stock
- 2 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tsp dark soy sauce
- 1 tsp sugar
- 2 tbsp chili oil with sediment

## Steps

1. Combine fish slices with egg white, cornstarch, and salt. Toss gently to coat and refrigerate for 15 minutes. This velvet coating keeps the fish silky when poached.
2. Blanch bean sprouts in boiling water for 1 minute; drain. Blanch greens for 30 seconds; drain. Arrange as a bed in a large, deep serving bowl.
3. Heat 3 tbsp oil in a wok over medium-high heat. Add doubanjiang and stir-fry for 2 minutes until the oil turns red and fragrant. Add garlic and ginger; stir-fry 1 more minute.
4. Pour in stock, soy sauce, dark soy sauce, and sugar. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a steady simmer. Cook for 5 minutes.
5. Gently add velveted fish slices one by one. Poach at a gentle simmer (do not boil) for 3–4 minutes until just cooked through and opaque. Do not stir — carefully swirl the pot instead.
6. Using a slotted spoon, carefully transfer fish on top of the vegetables in the serving bowl. Pour the hot broth over everything.
7. Scatter dried chilies, Sichuan peppercorns, and remaining minced garlic over the surface of the fish.
8. Heat remaining 2 tbsp oil and chili oil together in a small pan until smoking. Pour immediately over the aromatics on the fish — the sizzle blooms the final layer of flavour. Serve at the table straight from the bowl.

## Special Notice

> The defining finish of this dish is the hot-oil pour over the dried chilies and peppercorns at the very end — do not skip it. The dried chilies are for flavouring the oil and are not meant to be eaten whole. Shui zhu yu (water-boiled fish) is a Chongqing classic; its deceptive name refers to the poaching method, not the absence of oil and spice.
