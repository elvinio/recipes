# Braised Pork Belly with Bok Choy

> Serves 4 | 40 minutes | Chinese | dinner

## Ingredients

- 800 g pork belly, skin-on, cut into 4 cm cubes
- 4 baby bok choy, halved lengthwise
- 3 tbsp soy sauce
- 2 tbsp dark soy sauce
- 2 tbsp Shaoxing rice wine
- 2 tbsp sugar (or rock sugar)
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 300 mL water
- 6 cloves garlic, smashed
- 30 g fresh ginger, sliced
- 3 spring onions, tied in a knot
- 2 star anise
- 1 cinnamon stick
- 2 tsp neutral oil
- 800 g cooked jasmine rice, to serve

## Steps

1. Bring a pot of water to a boil. Add pork belly cubes and blanch for 5 minutes to remove impurities. Drain and pat dry with paper towels. Discard blanching water.
2. Set the electric pressure cooker to its sauté/browning function. Add oil and, working in batches, brown the pork belly on all sides until golden, about 4 minutes per batch. Remove and set aside.
3. Add garlic and ginger to the pot. Sauté for 1 minute. Add Shaoxing wine and let it bubble for 30 seconds to deglaze.
4. Return all pork to the pot. Add soy sauce, dark soy sauce, oyster sauce, sugar, water, spring onion knot, star anise, and cinnamon stick. Stir to combine.
5. Seal the pressure cooker lid and cook on high pressure for 20 minutes. Allow natural pressure release for 10 minutes, then manually release remaining pressure.
6. Open the lid. Remove and discard star anise, cinnamon, and spring onions. Set the pot to sauté/reduce and simmer the sauce, uncovered, for 5–8 minutes until it thickens to a glossy glaze.
7. While the sauce reduces, blanch bok choy halves in a separate pot of boiling salted water for 2 minutes; drain.
8. Arrange bok choy around the edge of a serving platter. Spoon pork and sauce into the centre. Serve with steamed rice.

## Special Notice

> Blanching the pork belly before browning removes blood and off-flavours, resulting in a cleaner, more refined braise. The pressure cooker dramatically shortens the traditional hong shao rou (red-braised pork) cook time without sacrificing the meltingly tender texture. Store leftovers with the braising liquid — the sauce solidifies when cold and can be reheated gently as a silky glaze.
